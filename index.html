Drunk Little Shit
04/23/14

     I am still the same as I was in highschool. A drunk little shit who thinks he/she/it is too good for everyone. A procrastinator. An asshole. A person that needs alcohol to make a post like this. A person who can't even make a post about shame for over a month, no matter how many times they look at it, work on it, and change it.

     I could reflect on the fact that even when drunk I can make blog posts that are readable, in HTML, but that's ridiculous. It's not of any skill to be able to post something in a template that was written earlier. I could reflect on my friends and family, of which I really have none. Those I had in the past are gone and those I have currently are just as hopelessly afloat as I am. I could post that stupid post about being ashamed of myself, everything I do, and everything I ever hope to do, but it's a ridiculous post, lamenting that I am a jerk who needs to be reminded he is appreciated on a regular basis.

     I could shoot myself. I would have to go and buy a firearm then, but is that all it takes? Is it really that easy? That is such a small investment for the end of one's life. This is ridiculous. I make everything bigger than it is. Dramatize it. Like a school production or english teacher fawning over a particular book or novel in an effort to grab the attention of various students.

     I feel like I have not changed. I still hate myself for caring so much. I still hate this reality I inhabit. I still hate people for what could only be briefly described as being human. I still hate myself for not feeling human. I hate myself for creating my own terrors and depression.

     Ugh. I have hurt and destroyed myself enough. I will try to get things done and turn myself around. Again.

A Queer End
03/18/14

   

     If you have not yet played Anna Anthropy's Queers in Love at The End of The World, then I suggest you do so now before continuing to read. Play it a few times, as only so much can be gleaned from quick, ten-second intervals of gameplay. Certainly, playing it once will be intriguing enough to keep you enthralled for a few fast-paced moments as you try to make the 'end of the world' worth something. If playing a quick game that will take up ten seconds of your time and offer an interesting perspective on decision-making does not interest you then please read on. before you do, though, please consider that even glancing at all these jumbled words will take longer than simply playing the game.

     In short, Queers in Love at The End of The World is comprised entirely of dramatic text and a ten-second timer that ticks down rapidly. Choices are made by clicking on blue text that resembles actions. It may appear simple but manages to encapsulate the essence of being in love in a manner that many longer, more drawn out games will fail to deliver. Each action and choice has much more weight to it by forcing the player to make multiple decisions within a time limit. You may find yourself playing the game multiple times, quickly spending more than a mere ten seconds trying to comprehend not only the deeply emotional sentences that appear on the screen with each frantic click, but the emptiness those sentences will incite when the end arrives.

     Before things fall apart and fade from the screen, each tastefully revealing click is entirely dependent on where the player wants to go and what they want to accomplish. I found myself quickly scanning and muttering the words as my partner waited for me to acknowledge them in a way that would not actually mean anything when everything is wiped away. Which is an odd feeling, since the relationship isn't actually defined. There are very few specific details about the relationship that you share with this significant other, but by keeping specifics to a minimum and employing a text-only tale the player is able to better imagine their interpretation of a loved one.

     Despite there being no graphic representation of the deep ardour being displayed on screen the way wording is employed makes the game play heartwrenchingly well, leaving the detail to the player as they may recollect their own relationships or idealistic ideas of intimacy. No matter what choices are made, though, it will all come to an abrupt end in a fairly quick and almost quiet manner. The game is sad in the way that it makes each moment more tangible than the last in a desperate attempt to make some action be noteworthy. By making each choice such an important factor to the mere ten seconds you have to play, each decision has a particular weight and complexity that is then rendered useless when the end arrives. It is a very interesting design i would love to see explored in more detail.

     While ruminating on the idea that we may need more games with a timed dialogue mechanic in them, I will again suggest that at this point you go ahead and try Queers in Love at The End of The World. If I have not yet convinced you to at least try the game then I could resort to begging. Please try it. Preferably alone, comfortably, with a cup of wine and a morbid mood. You will not regret it.
